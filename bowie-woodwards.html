The Woodwards

William Woodward’s racing stable, the Triple Crown bloodlines bred at Belair, the 1955 shooting that ended the era, and the estate sale that cleared the way for suburban Bowie.

Belair Stud · 1917–1955

The Woodward family at Belair

William Woodward Sr. · acquired 1917 Belair Stud Triple Crown dynasty

William Woodward acquired Belair in the period 1917–1920 and transformed it into one of the most prominent racing stables in America. Under his stewardship, Belair Stud produced the only father-and-son pair of Triple Crown winners in history — Gallant Fox in 1930 and Omaha in 1935 — both trained by the legendary Sunny Jim Fitzsimmons. Granville, another Belair horse, was named Horse of the Year in 1936. The horses, their bloodlines, and the track records are covered in detail in Chapter 3: Racing & Horses.

The Woodward chapter at Belair represents the culmination of a thread that runs from Governor Samuel Ogle’s 18th-century English imports through two centuries of careful breeding on the same Maryland ground. When William Woodward Jr. took over the stable, it was already one of the great American racing operations. The year that ended it — 1955 — was also, almost perversely, the year it reached its peak.

A dramatic coda · October 1955

Ann Woodward and Deliberate Cruelty

October 1955 Ann Woodward Deliberate Cruelty · Montillo, 2022

In October 1955 — weeks after Nashua’s nationally broadcast triumph over Swaps in Chicago — Ann Woodward shot William Woodward Jr. at their Long Island estate. She maintained it was an accident; she believed an intruder had entered the house. The case became one of the most sensational society scandals of the postwar era. Truman Capote reportedly based the character of Holly Golightly in part on Ann Woodward, and later published a thinly veiled portrait of her that she found devastating.

Roseanne Montillo’s Deliberate Cruelty: Truman Capote, the Millionaire’s Wife, and the Murder of the Century (2022) revisits the case in full, tracing both the shooting and Capote’s long campaign to destroy Ann Woodward’s reputation. The book is the most thorough recent account of the story. The reason it belongs in a history of Bowie is direct: the Woodwards were owners of the Belair estate and one of the most prominent racing stables in the country, and the collapse of that era — the scandal, the estate sale — helped clear the way for suburban Bowie as it exists today.

Sources: New York Times review of Deliberate Cruelty · Wikipedia: Ann Woodward
Interesting aside · source: oral history

The White House sheep and the Woodward farm

One particularly colorful story, attributed to curators at the Woodrow Wilson Presidential Library in Staunton during a museum visit, links the White House sheep — purchased during WWI for wartime conservation symbolism — to William Woodward’s farm in Bowie. President Wilson had a flock of sheep installed on the White House grounds to graze the lawn, saving labor for the war effort and generating wool that was auctioned for the Red Cross.

I learned about this connection while visiting the Wilson Library as part of a Virginia historical tour. When I mentioned I was from Bowie, MD, a staff member asked whether I recognized the Woodward name — noting that when they talk about the sheep with visitors, they tell people the sheep came from the Woodwards and from Bowie. The name rang a bell, but it took me a moment to place it. Once I made the connection to Belair Mansion, I mentioned that William Woodward had been killed by his wife — which prompted the staff to look it up on the spot.

I also let them know that the Belair Mansion is now a city museum and suggested they might want to reach out to the City of Bowie Museum staff — two small museums with an unexpected shared story seem like a natural fit for some kind of coordination.

This connection rests on informed staff attribution rather than published institutional material. It is worth noting as a plausible claim with a good-faith source, but should be treated as oral history pending a stronger published citation.

Sources: White House sheep — Daily Dose Documentary
End of an era · 1957

The Levitt sale — horse country becomes a suburb

1957 purchase Levitt & Sons Belair at Bowie

Two years after the shooting, Levitt & Sons purchased the Belair estate and announced plans to build a large planned residential community on the former farm and racing grounds. The same year that produced Belair Stud’s greatest champion — 1955 — had already ended the stable, the family, and the estate’s identity as horse country. The Levitt purchase in 1957 closed the chapter entirely.

The estate that Governor Samuel Ogle built in 1745, that sheltered two governors and an artillery train bound for Yorktown, that produced the foundation mare of American thoroughbred racing and the only father-son Triple Crown dynasty in history, became the site of what would eventually be called Belair at Bowie — one of the largest planned suburbs in the mid-Atlantic. The mansion itself survived; it is now a City of Bowie museum. The story of what Levitt built there, and at what cost, continues in Chapter 5: Modern Bowie.
