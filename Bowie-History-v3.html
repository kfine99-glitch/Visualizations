Colonial estate

Belair Mansion and the Ogle governors

The estate that anchors Bowie's entire historical identity — colonial, equestrian, and suburban.

1745 · The mansion rises

Two governors, one house

Belair Mansion was built around 1745–1747 for Governor Samuel Ogle and Anne Tasker Ogle. Local histories and museum material describe it as the home of two colonial governors because of its Ogle family associations, including Governor Benjamin Ogle. The mansion's Georgian architecture and surviving outbuildings form the physical core of what became the Woodward racing stable in the 20th century and is now a City of Bowie museum.

1781 · The Yorktown campaign

Rochambeau's column passes Belair

City of Bowie historical material records that a large French wagon-and-artillery column passed Belair Mansion in September 1781 during the Yorktown campaign, departing from Easton's Plantation and moving south along what corresponds to today's Route 450. The Bowie Living post reproducing that material describes approximately 200 wagons, artillery, and roughly 3,600 oxen and horses.

This is among the most dramatic moments in the estate's early history — the mansion sitting along the route of the campaign that ended the Revolutionary War.

Railroad era

From Huntington to Bowie — rails and junctions

The railroad geometry explains why Bowie mattered at all, and why it became a race-day destination for three cities at once.

1853–1880 · The B&P Railroad

A junction town named for its founder

Col. William D. Bowie persuaded the Maryland legislature to charter the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad in 1853. Oden Bowie became railroad president and later governor of Maryland. The junction that opened in 1872 generated a settlement called Huntington City around Bowie Station; the town was renamed Bowie in 1880 in his honor.

Historic rail accounts describe the B&P's original main line as the route from Baltimore south toward Pope's Creek on the Potomac. At the Bowie junction the company created a Washington branch — which ultimately became the main passenger route into Washington used by the Pennsylvania Railroad and, today, Amtrak's Northeast Corridor.

The WB&A Electric Railway

Three cities, one interurban

The City of Bowie traces the race track's founding location partly to the Washington, Baltimore & Annapolis Electric Railway — the interurban that connected all three cities and ran right past the chosen site. A 1949 Baltimore Evening Sun profile of track superintendent Richard Pending confirms that George Osbelt, assistant superintendent of the WB&A, personally helped select the Bowie site because the railroad could add a spur line from its main track.

Race-day WB&A service ran from Baltimore with round-trip fares around 65 cents. After the WB&A collapsed in the 1930s, Pennsylvania Railroad service continued to the track by another route. The old interurban right-of-way is now the WB&A Trail, with the Patuxent River bridge connecting Prince George's and Anne Arundel County segments fully opened in 2025.

Huntington City · What remains

A town more ambitious than what survives

Rail history and local summaries describe Huntington City as a more substantial town than what exists today — hotels, stores, and activity clustered around the station. Repeated fires and the later shift of development away from the original railroad core explain why Huntington now feels like a fragment. The Bowie Railroad Museum and the surviving station buildings preserve the physical memory of the junction district.

1853 · The Washington branch play

The DC spur became the big prize

Because the Baltimore and Ohio Railroad opposed a direct Pennsylvania Railroad route into Washington, the B&P charter's branch-line language became the workaround. By building through Bowie and branching into Washington, the railroad gained the connection that became the dominant north-south passenger approach to the capital — the same line that feeds Amtrak into Union Station today. The Bowie junction was the geographic hinge of that strategy.

Horse country

Cradle of American horseracing — 71 years at the track

The imported bloodstock of the 18th century, the founding of the track in 1914, and the anecdotes that define its memory.

1914 · Prince George's Park opens

Selecting the Bowie site

A 1949 Baltimore Evening Sun profile of track superintendent Richard J. "Dick" Pending recounts how he and his associates — particularly Jim O'Hara — persuaded officials to look in Prince George's County after earlier success at Marlboro. They located the site at Bowie through George Osbelt of the WB&A Railroad, who recognized that the railroad could add a spur directly from its main line.

"Bryan and O'Hara bought the place and put up a 3,000-seat grandstand without having to buy a piece of lumber. The stand was all wood; we just chopped down the trees, set up a sawmill and went to work."

Richard J. Pending, quoted in the Baltimore Evening Sun, c. 1949

The first meeting was a near-disaster: the New York Jockey Club refused to license the track, so organizers obtained dates from the Prince George's County Circuit Court and opened in October, directly in competition with Laurel. The first day drew sixteen bookmakers paying $100 a day each. Within two days that number had dropped to four.

Fan memories · The closing article

When Bowie goes — Norman Perrin remembers

At the track's closing, the Baltimore Evening Sun published fan recollections from Norman Perrin, who had been attending since 1933 and was approaching 75. His memories of arriving via the two-car WB&A train — heated baggage car, passengers clustered around a wood-burning stove on cold Thanksgiving-season Saturdays — capture the texture of Depression-era racing culture at Bowie.

"The lead car in the two-car train was a powered baggage car. There were plain wooden benches crosswise in the car, and there was an egg stove [wood-burning stove] in the middle of the car. Most of the customers were clustered around the stove, since this was the Saturday before or just after Thanksgiving, and it was cold, with small snow flurries."

Norman Perrin, quoted in "Memories of Bowie to linger long after storied track closes," Baltimore Evening Sun

Perrin also recalled a Christmas horse story: on the same day, the big 2-year-old race was won by a horse named Snowflake, and a preceding race featured sprinters named Eskimo and Igloo. He noted an outside betting window where patrons could wager without paying track admission — a feature that, he assumed, served the train crew and fans who had money to bet but not enough for admission.

1958 blizzard · "When it snows, Bowie goes"

3,000 patrons stranded overnight

The City of Bowie's horse-racing history recalls a 1958 blizzard that stranded roughly 3,000 patrons overnight at the track. Blocked roads left fans sleeping in the clubhouse, barns, and cars. The episode gave rise to the local line "When it snows, Bowie goes" — a phrase that captured both the track's reputation for bad-weather stubbornness and the peculiar loyalty of its regular crowd.

March 1959 · A famous visit

Nixon and Hoover at Bowie

Vice President Richard Nixon visited Bowie Race Track with FBI Director J. Edgar Hoover in March 1959 for Tricia Nixon's birthday outing. A Bowie Living post documents the visit, which illustrates the track's reach as a regional institution that drew national figures during the Eisenhower era.

"Long after Bowie is gone, just like at Havre de Grace, a murmur on the wind can be heard echoing the cry of the losing bettor: 'I was gonna' or 'If I'd a . . .'"

Norman Perrin's closing letter, quoted in the Baltimore Evening Sun

Primary source archive

Baltimore Evening Sun — racetrack clippings

Two newspaper articles that provide firsthand accounts of Bowie Race Track's founding and its closing chapter.

"Memories of Bowie to linger long after storied track closes"

By Dale Austin · Baltimore Evening Sun · c. 1985

Fan Norman Perrin's recollections at the track's closing after 71 years of racing. Perrin, who first visited in 1933, describes arriving on the WB&A two-car train, the wood-burning stove in the heated baggage car, and the "Christmas horse" races of that cold Thanksgiving-season Saturday. The article notes that dates were redistributed to Laurel and Pimlico, and that Perrin had stopped attending when year-round racing at Charles Town made Bowie feel unnecessary. His closing letter gives the article its elegiac final note.

"Pending, 56 Years In Saddle As Rider And Trainer, Helped Select Bowie Site"

By Snowden Carter · Baltimore Evening Sun · c. 1949

A profile of Richard J. Pending, track superintendent, who entered racing in 1893 at age 13 after walking from his home in downtown New York to the Morris Park track in Westchester. The article covers his role in selecting the Bowie site, the improvised grandstand built entirely from trees felled on the property, the early conflict with the New York Jockey Club, and the first-day bookmaker count. Pending recalls buying 420 acres near the track after it opened for $5,000 — land now covered by parking lots and stables.

Chronology

Key moments in Bowie history

From Belair's construction to Levitt-era suburban growth — and the track's closing chapter.

1745–1747

Belair Mansion rises

Constructed for Governor Samuel Ogle and Anne Tasker Ogle, creating the estate that anchors Bowie's colonial, equestrian, and suburban story for the next two and a half centuries.

1781

Rochambeau's column passes

A French wagon-and-artillery train — roughly 200 wagons, artillery, and 3,600 oxen and horses — passes Belair on the Yorktown campaign route, moving south along today's Route 450.

1853

Baltimore and Potomac Railroad chartered

William D. Bowie secures the railroad charter; Oden Bowie becomes president, setting up the Bowie junction as the geographic pivot of the future Northeast Corridor.

1872–1880

Huntington becomes Bowie

The rail junction generates Huntington City around Bowie Station. Hotels, stores, and activity cluster near the tracks. The town is renamed Bowie in 1880 in honor of Oden Bowie.

1893

Richard Pending enters racing

Age 13, Dick Pending walks from downtown New York to the Morris Park track in Westchester and is hired by trainer Mark Burns. He will spend 56 years in the saddle before becoming Bowie's track superintendent.

1914

Bowie Race Track opens

Prince George's Park opens — trees felled on the property to build the 3,000-seat grandstand. The New York Jockey Club refuses to license the meet; dates are obtained from the Prince George's County Circuit Court instead. Sixteen bookmakers pay $100 a day on opening day.

1917–1920

Woodward era begins at Belair

William Woodward acquires Belair and transforms it into one of the most prominent racing stables in America. A colorful secondary-source anecdote connects the Woodward farm to Woodrow Wilson's White House sheep, purchased for wartime conservation symbolism.

Daily Dose Documentary: White House sheep

1933

Norman Perrin's first visit

Fan Norman Perrin makes his first trip to Bowie on the WB&A train — the cold, crowded baggage car with the wood-burning stove, the Christmas horses, the $23 payoff. He will remember it vividly 50 years later when the track closes.

1955–1957

Woodward scandal and sale

Ann Woodward shoots William Woodward Jr. in 1955 in a case later revisited in Roseanne Montillo's book Deliberate Cruelty. Two years later Levitt & Sons buy the Belair estate, ending the Woodward chapter and beginning suburban Bowie.

1958

"When it snows, Bowie goes"

A December blizzard strands roughly 3,000 patrons overnight at the track, sleeping in the clubhouse, barns, and cars. The phrase enters local memory as the emblem of Bowie's weather-defying racing culture.

1959–1960s

Levitt builds Belair at Bowie

Levitt & Sons develop the Belair estate into a planned suburban community, ultimately building thousands of homes and transforming Bowie into one of the largest planned suburbs in the mid-Atlantic. Civil rights protests challenge Levitt's discriminatory sales practices.

1985

Track closes after 71 years

Bowie Race Track closes. Racing dates are redistributed to Laurel and Pimlico. The Baltimore Evening Sun publishes Dale Austin's closing-day article with Norman Perrin's memories — and his elegiac final note that somewhere in the wind you can still hear the losing bettor: "I was gonna" or "If I'd a . . ."

A dramatic coda

The Woodwards and Deliberate Cruelty

The Woodward story is not merely social history — it explains why Belair passed out of the family's hands and into Levitt's.

Book connection

Why this story belongs here

Roseanne Montillo's Deliberate Cruelty: Truman Capote, the Millionaire's Wife, and the Murder of the Century (2022) revisits the 1955 shooting of William Woodward Jr. by Ann Woodward. The Woodwards were owners of the Belair estate and one of the most prominent racing stables in the country. The collapse of that era — the scandal, the estate sale — directly produced suburban Bowie as we know it.

New York Times review · Wikipedia: Ann Woodward

Interesting aside

The White House sheep anecdote

One particularly colorful story, attributed to curators at the Woodrow Wilson Presidential Library in Staunton during a museum visit, links the White House sheep — purchased for wartime conservation symbolism during WWI — to William Woodward's Bowie farm. This connection rests on informed staff attribution rather than published institutional material. It is worth noting as a plausible claim with a good-faith source, but should be treated as oral history pending a stronger published citation.

Suburban transformation

Levitt & Sons — Belair at Bowie

The postwar development that turned horse country into one of the mid-Atlantic's largest planned suburbs.

1957 · The purchase

From racing stable to suburb

After the Woodward era, Levitt & Sons acquired the Belair estate in 1957 and built Belair at Bowie — the planned community that gave modern Bowie its dominant residential form. Levitt applied the same assembly-line construction techniques used at Levittown, New York and Levittown, Pennsylvania, producing thousands of homes on the former farm and racing stable grounds.

Civil rights · Levitt's discrimination

Protests against discriminatory sales

Levitt & Sons practiced racially discriminatory sales policies at Belair at Bowie, as they had at their other developments. Civil rights protesters challenged the company's practices, and 8mm home footage from the period — uploaded from Paul Flaherty's original film — documents both the early community and the protest activity. The civil rights struggle at Bowie is part of the broader Prince George's County story of racial change in the postwar suburban boom.
