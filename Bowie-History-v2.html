Railroad era

From Huntington to Bowie

Col. William D. Bowie pushed for the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad charter in 1853, while Oden Bowie served as railroad president and later governor; the junction that opened in 1872 helped create Huntington, later renamed Bowie in 1880.

Railroad origin story Oden Bowie

Colonial estate

Belair Mansion and two governors

Belair Mansion was built around 1745 for Governor Samuel Ogle and Anne Tasker Ogle. Local histories and museum material treat it as the home of two colonial governors because of its Ogle family associations, including Governor Benjamin Ogle.

Horse country

Cradle of American horseracing

The City of Bowie traces Bowie’s racing importance to the imported bloodstock of the 18th century, especially Selima, and to the later prominence of the Belair stable and Bowie Race Track, which opened in 1914.

Selima Bowie Race Track

Two especially vivid racetrack anecdotes deserve mention here. City of Bowie history recalls the 1958 blizzard that stranded about 3,000 patrons overnight at the track and helped inspire the local line “When it snows, Bowie goes.” A Bowie Living article adds that the heavy snow and blocked roads left fans sleeping in the clubhouse, barns, and even cars. A later Baltimore newspaper feature on the track’s closing includes fan Norman Perrin’s memories of arriving in a two-car WB&A train with a heated baggage car and riders clustering around the wood-burning stove on cold Thanksgiving-season Saturdays. A separate Bowie Living post notes that Vice President Richard Nixon visited Bowie Race Track with J. Edgar Hoover in March 1959 for Tricia Nixon’s birthday outing.

Interurban era

The Washington, Baltimore & Annapolis Railroad

The City of Bowie notes that Bowie Race Track was chosen in part because it sat near Baltimore, Annapolis, Washington, and the electric railroad serving all three. That line was the Washington, Baltimore & Annapolis Electric Railway, the interurban whose former right-of-way later became the Washington, Baltimore and Annapolis Trail.

A 1949 profile of long‑time track superintendent Richard J. Pending in the Baltimore Evening Sun recounts how he and other officials deliberately scouted Prince George’s County for a new track and settled on farmland along the WB&A, working with one of the railroad’s assistant superintendents to choose the exact Bowie site because the company could add a spur in from the main line.

Accounts of Bowie Race Track say the WB&A ran frequent race-day service from Baltimore, with round-trip fares of about 65 cents, making Bowie an easy outing for city residents. After the WB&A collapsed in the 1930s, Pennsylvania Railroad service continued to the track by another route.

Anne Arundel County announced that the new Patuxent River bridge opened to the public in May 2025 and was formally celebrated that September, creating nearly 13 miles of continuous trail between the Prince George’s and Anne Arundel sections. That gives today’s trail users a more complete physical link to the old interurban corridor you used to ride.

The often-repeated story that Baltimore patrons also rode down to Bowie to buy cheaper liquor in Prince George’s County may well reflect local memory of excursion culture, and you recalled possibly seeing it on interpretive signs at the trail parking area, but I still have not located a solid published source for that claim, so it should stay framed as oral history unless we find one.

Suburban transformation

Levitt at Bowie

After the Woodward era, Levitt & Sons acquired the Belair estate in 1957 and built Belair at Bowie, the planned suburban development that gave modern Bowie its dominant residential form.

Chronology

Key moments

A compact timeline from Belair’s construction to Levitt-era suburban growth.

1745–1747

Belair Mansion rises

Belair Mansion is constructed for Samuel Ogle and Anne Tasker Ogle, creating the estate that later anchors much of Bowie’s colonial, equestrian, and suburban story.

1781

Rochambeau’s logistics pass Belair

The Bowie Living post reproducing City of Bowie material says a major French wagon-and-artillery train passed Belair Mansion in September 1781 on the Yorktown campaign route after leaving Easton’s Plantation. The article describes about 200 wagons, artillery, and roughly 3,600 oxen and horses moving south along what corresponds to today’s Route 450.

Bowie Living post

1853

Railroad chartered

City of Bowie history says William D. Bowie persuaded the legislature to charter the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad and that Oden Bowie became its president, laying the groundwork for the future town.

1872–1880

Huntington becomes Bowie

The rail junction develops into Huntington City around Bowie Station; the town is renamed Bowie in 1880 in honor of Oden Bowie. Local memory of fires and later decline helps explain why so little of the once more ambitious downtown survives.

1914

Bowie Race Track opens

Prince George’s Park, later Bowie Race Track, opens and connects the town’s rail access with its horse-racing prominence.

1917–1920

White House sheep story

A modern retelling says Woodrow Wilson’s White House sheep came from William Woodward’s Bowie farm, where the flock was purchased for wartime conservation symbolism. This is colorful and plausible, but it should be treated as a secondary-source anecdote unless a stronger contemporary citation is added later.

Daily Dose Documentary article

1955–1957

Woodward scandal and sale

Ann Woodward shot William Woodward Jr. in 1955, a case later revisited in Roseanne Montillo’s book Deliberate Cruelty. Two years later Levitt & Sons bought the Belair estate, ending the Woodward chapter and beginning suburban Bowie.

A dramatic coda

The Woodwards and Deliberate Cruelty

The Woodward story is not just gossip history; it helps explain why Belair passed out of that family’s hands.

Book connection

Why this story belongs here

Roseanne Montillo’s Deliberate Cruelty: Truman Capote, the Millionaire’s Wife, and the Murder of the Century revisits the 1955 shooting of William Woodward Jr. by Ann Woodward. The scandal belongs in a Bowie history page because the Woodwards were tied to Belair, and the collapse of that era was followed by the 1957 sale to Levitt & Sons.

New York Times review · Book coverage · Ann Woodward

Interesting aside

The White House sheep anecdote

One particularly colorful story, shared by curators at the Woodrow Wilson Presidential Library in Staunton, links the White House sheep to William Woodward’s Bowie farm; while this connection is based on museum staff attribution rather than the library’s published web materials, it is worth noting as an informed but not fully published claim. That gives the anecdote a better footing than a casual internet rumor, but it still is not the same thing as a published institutional citation.

Railroad deep dive

Pope’s Creek, Washington, and Huntington’s unrealized future

The early railroad geometry explains why Bowie mattered so much in the first place.

Original line

The Pope’s Creek line came first

Historic rail accounts describe the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad’s original main line as the route from Baltimore south toward Pope’s Creek on the Potomac. At the junction near Bowie, the company created a Washington branch, and that branch ultimately became the main passenger route into Washington used by the Pennsylvania Railroad and later the Northeast Corridor.

Prince George’s County Tricentennial history · Wikipedia: Pope’s Creek Subdivision · Wikipedia: Baltimore and Potomac Railroad

Washington branch

The DC spur became the big prize

Because the Baltimore and Ohio opposed a direct Pennsylvania Railroad route into Washington, the Baltimore and Potomac charter’s branch-line language became the workaround. By building the line through Bowie and then branching into Washington, the railroad gained the connection that later became the dominant north-south passenger approach to the capital and today feeds Amtrak service in and out of Washington.

Huntington City

A town that could have been larger

Rail history and local historical summaries describe Huntington City as a more ambitious town than what survives today, with hotels, stores, and activity clustered around the station. The old downtown’s repeated fires and the later shift of development away from the original railroad core help explain why Huntington now feels like a fragment of a place that once had larger regional ambitions.

From Huntington to Bowie marker · City of Bowie history

What remains

The shell of the old rail town

Today the railroad buildings and museum preserve the physical memory of Huntington’s station district even though the community never became the major DC-area center it might once have seemed poised to become. That contrast between promise and survival is part of what makes old Bowie historically compelling.
