Solar System Explorer

Interactive heliocentric simulator that lets you orbit the Sun alongside the planets, moons, and deep-space probes to feel the real scale, timing, and geometry of the solar system.

Includes all eight planets with realistic orbital periods and rotation, 14 key moons, and 12 historic probes from Pioneer and Voyager through New Horizons, Juno, Dawn, Rosetta, and Parker Solar Probe. *

Time controls let you play, pause, reverse, and change simulation speed, use date search to jump to any calendar date, and focus the camera on any object while toggling orbits, moons, and probe trajectories.

Washington–Rochambeau March to Yorktown, 1781

Visualizes the 680-mile Franco-American march from Providence, Rhode Island to Yorktown, Virginia — the largest and most complex coordinated military movement of the Revolutionary War, culminating in Cornwallis's surrender on October 19, 1781.

Clearly shows the six parallel elements of the march (Admiral de Grasse's French fleet converging from the Caribbean, Lauzun’s Legion cavalry riding 10–15 miles ahead as advance guard; Washington’s independent party traveling with the main army to Baltimore before riding ahead independent; the main allied army, Annapolis water route, and Du Plessis artillery and wagon train — ~200 wagons, 3,600 oxen, and 1,355 horses — escorted overland through Bowie, Maryland). It highlights the “Great Split” at Baltimore and includes rich historical context drawn from primary sources.

61 camps georeferenced against primary sources including the Berthier journal maps (Princeton), the NPS Washington–Rochambeau Revolutionary Route, W3R Association state surveys, the Reyes Maryland survey (2001), and Washington's own diary. Camp detail panels include dates, historical notes, and direct links to Wikipedia, W3R-US encampment pages, and Historical Marker Database entries. A live Leaflet map zooms to each selected location.

Civil War Prisoner of War Camps, 1861–1865

Comparative institutional timeline of 22 major Union and Confederate prison and parole camps, showing each camp's operational period, peak population, and mortality rate — from Johnson's Island (2.7%, officers' club atmosphere) to Andersonville (28.7%, nearly 13,000 dead in 14 months) and Elmira (24.3%, "Hellmira").

Bar width represents operational duration; bar color encodes mortality rate from cool blue through deep red. The story turns on a single mechanism: the Dix-Hill prisoner exchange cartel kept camps nearly empty for a year — until it collapsed in mid-1863 when the Confederacy refused to treat Black Union soldiers as legitimate prisoners of war, followed by Grant's deliberate 1864 decision to suspend all exchanges as strategic policy. *

Local Maryland anchors highlighted: Point Lookout (St. Mary's County) was the largest Union POW camp by peak population, holding 20,000 Confederate prisoners in tents at the tip of the Chesapeake. Camp Parole (now the Annapolis suburb still called Parole) held Union soldiers released on parole and awaiting exchange — Clara Barton's wartime headquarters. Click any camp bar or map marker for a detail panel with full history and primary source links.

American Military Conflicts

417-year Gantt-style timeline of 88 named armed conflicts involving the American colonies, territories, and United States — from the Anglo-Powhatan Wars and Pequot War through both World Wars, Korea, Vietnam, and the ongoing anti-ISIS campaign. Bar width represents actual conflict duration; bar height scales with conflict magnitude across five tiers from civilization-defining wars down to border skirmishes.

Nine military eras (Colonial through Post-Cold War/GWOT), color-coded by adversary type across six categories. Includes rarely-documented conflicts: the Maryland–Virginia Border Wars (1635), Pueblo Revolt (1680), Stono Rebellion (1739), War of Jenkins' Ear — Georgia Campaign (1739), Shays' Rebellion (1786), and Bleeding Kansas (1854–59). 36 conflict-chain connections rendered as bezier curves trace causal relationships across the timeline — clicking any bar lights up its chain and lists connected conflicts in the detail panel. *

Detail panels provide dates, theater, forces engaged, casualties, commanders, outcome, and a direct link to the Wikipedia article for every conflict. Many panels — particularly conflicts involving Native American nations — include a curated Further Exploration section with links to National Park Service historic sites, official tribal nation websites, federal historical offices, and research centers. The tribal nation links are especially significant: sites such as the Oglala Sioux Tribe, the Seminole Tribe of Florida, the Nez Perce Tribe (Nimiipuu), and the Navajo Nation present these conflicts in their own voice, documenting perspectives, losses, and ongoing consequences that standard historical accounts often minimize or omit. For any conflict involving Indigenous peoples, the tribal link is the most valuable research resource in the panel. *

Filters for adversary type, military era, and outcome; a year filter that dims all conflicts not active in a given year; a Jump dropdown; ◀ Era / Era ▶ buttons to step through eras; ★ Surprise and ✦ Discover buttons (six smart navigation modes including US Defeats, Deadliest Conflicts, Most Chain-Connected, and Not Yet Visited); and full-text search.

Bowie History Atlas

A source-linked visual history of Bowie, Maryland — from the 1681 Catton patent and colonial Belair Mansion through the railroad era, the founding of American thoroughbred racing, the Woodward scandal, Levitt's suburban buildout, forced busing, and the city's modern institutions.

Sections cover: colonial Belair and the "House of Governors"; slavery and resistance at Belair; the B&P Railroad junction that created the town; Selima and the 1752 race that ignited American thoroughbred racing; 71 years at Bowie Race Track; Gallant Fox, Omaha, and Nashua at Belair Stud; the Woodward murder case; Levitt & Sons and the postwar suburb; forced busing 1973–2001 (the largest such program in the nation); Bowie State University (Maryland's oldest HBCU); Six Flags America through all five iterations; Belt Woods old-growth forest conservation; the city's three successive City Halls; the Bowie Police Department; and notable residents including Eva Cassidy, Paul Reed Smith, Ricky Arnold, and JC Chasez.

Personal memoir woven throughout — the page's author is a lifelong Bowie resident who lived through the busing era, attended Six Flags America through every one of its five iterations, and walks the streets where this history happened.
